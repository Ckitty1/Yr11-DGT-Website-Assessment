History

The history of Alabastor is a tale of accidental brilliance, beginning in 1742 when the merchant vessel The Gilded Petal was blown off course during a ferocious Pacific gale. As the storm broke, Captain Alistair Finch sighted what he initially believed to be a mountain of salt rising from the waves; however, upon landing, he discovered high cliffs of a rare, translucent mineral that captured and softened the morning sun.


Awed by the terrain, Finch noted in his log that the island was "a cathedral of stone," and he christened the land Alabastor, a stylized nod to the alabaster rock that remains the nation's most precious resource. While the island was already home to a small, secluded community of indigenous navigators who called the land "The Glowing Heart," the name Alabastor stuck on international charts. Over the centuries, the settlers and locals blended their cultures, building the luminous white cities that define the island's skyline today, forever linking the nation's identity to the stone that saved a lost captain.
